Most common threat

Recognizing Phishing Emails

Phishing emails are fake messages designed to trick you into giving up passwords, clicking harmful links, or sending money. They often look very real. Here's how to spot them.

⚠️
Key rule
If an email creates urgency, threatens consequences, or asks for sensitive information — slow down and verify before acting.

Red flag example — hover the numbered badges to see what's wrong:

⚠ Phishing email example
From: IT-Support@microsofft-helpdesk.com 1Misspelled domain — not Microsoft
To: you@yourcompany.com
Subject: URGENT: Your account will be suspended in 24 hours 2Urgency + threats = pressure tactic

Dear Valued Employee, 3Generic greeting — not your name


We have detected suspicious activity on your account. You must verify your credentials immediately or your account will be permanently deleted. 4Threatening consequences to create panic


Click here to verify now: http://secure-microsoft-login.xyz/verify 5Suspicious URL — not a real Microsoft domain


Regards,
Microsoft Support Team 6No contact info, no official signature

What a legitimate IT email looks like:

✓ Legitimate email example
From: helpdesk@yourcompany.com ✓Official company domain
To: Jane Smith
Subject: Password reset confirmation ✓Calm, specific subject

Hi Jane, ✓Uses your actual name


Your password was successfully reset. If you did not request this change, please contact IT at extension 4400 or reply to this email. ✓Gives you a way to verify independently


No action is needed if you made this change yourself.


— IT Help Desk, Your Company
helpdesk@yourcompany.com | ext. 4400 ✓Real contact information

🚩 Red flags

  • Sender address doesn't match the company
  • Generic greeting ("Dear Customer")
  • Urgent threats or deadlines
  • Requests for passwords or payment
  • Suspicious or misspelled links
  • Unexpected attachments

✅ Good signs

  • Sender matches a known company domain
  • Uses your real name
  • No pressure to act immediately
  • Provides a way to verify independently
  • Clear contact info in signature
  • Grammar and spelling are correct
02
Before you click

Suspicious Links & Attachments

Links and file attachments are the two most common ways malware gets onto computers. Here's what to check before you ever click.

💡
Hover before you click
On a computer, hover your mouse over any link and look at the bottom of your browser window — it shows the real destination URL before you visit it.

How to read a URL:

https://paypal.com/login/verify ✅ Safe — correct domain, secure (https)
http://paypal-secure-login.xyz/verify 🚩 Suspicious — fake domain, no https, strange extension (.xyz)
🚫
Never open these attachment types from unknown senders
.exe  ·  .zip (unexpected)  ·  .docm / .xlsm (macro-enabled Office files)  ·  .js  ·  .bat  ·  .vbs  — When in doubt, contact the sender through a separate channel to confirm they sent it.
✅
Generally safe to open from trusted senders
.pdf  ·  .docx  ·  .xlsx  ·  .png / .jpg — Still be cautious if you weren't expecting the file.
03
Your first line of defense

Password Security

Weak or reused passwords are one of the top causes of account breaches. Creating strong passwords doesn't have to be complicated.

Example password Strength Why
password123 Weak Common word + predictable numbers
Tr0ub4dor! Medium Substitutions are easy for attackers to guess
correct-horse-battery-staple Strong 4 random words — long, memorable, hard to crack
xK#8mP!qLz@2vN Strong Random characters — best used with a password manager
🔐
Use a password manager
Tools like Bitwarden (free), 1Password, or LastPass generate and remember strong passwords for every site so you only need to remember one master password.

❌ Never do this

  • Reuse the same password across sites
  • Use personal info (birthday, pet's name)
  • Share passwords via email or chat
  • Write passwords on sticky notes
  • Use passwords shorter than 12 characters

✅ Always do this

  • Use a unique password for every account
  • Make passwords at least 12–16 characters
  • Use a password manager
  • Change passwords if you suspect a breach
  • Enable MFA (see next section)
04
Extra layer of protection

Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

MFA means using a second step to verify it's really you when logging in — even if someone steals your password, they can't get in without your phone or device.

🔤 Step 1

Enter your username and password as usual

📱 Step 2

Approve a prompt on your phone or enter a short code

✅ You're in

Both steps verified — your account is protected

🚨
MFA fatigue attacks — know this
If you receive repeated MFA approval requests that you didn't trigger, do NOT approve them. This is an attacker hoping you'll click "approve" to make them stop. Report it to IT immediately.
📲
Best MFA methods (most to least secure)
1. Hardware security key (e.g. YubiKey)  ·  2. Authenticator app (Google Authenticator, Microsoft Authenticator)  ·  3. Push notification  ·  4. SMS text code — (avoid if possible; SIM swapping is a risk)
05
Everyday habits

Safe Browsing Practices

Most security incidents start with a single click. These habits take seconds and protect both you and your organization.

Daily browsing checklist — click to check off:

  • ✓
    Look for https:// and the padlock icon before entering any personal or login information
  • ✓
    Don't use public Wi-Fi for work tasks — use your company VPN or a personal hotspot instead
  • ✓
    Lock your computer (Windows+L or Ctrl+Command+Q on Mac) whenever you step away
  • ✓
    Keep your browser and operating system updated — updates patch security holes
  • ✓
    Don't install browser extensions or software without IT approval
  • ✓
    Use your work device for work, and personal devices for personal use — don't mix them
  • ✓
    Be skeptical of pop-ups that say your computer is infected — close them, don't click
06
When something feels wrong

When & How to Report

Reporting a suspicious email or incident is never an overreaction. The earlier IT knows about a threat, the faster they can protect everyone. Here's what to do:

1
Stop — don't click, reply, or forward

If something feels wrong, pause. Don't interact with the email, link, or file any further.

2
Report it to IT immediately

Use your company's "Report Phishing" button in Outlook/Gmail, or email/call the IT help desk directly. Don't worry about being wrong — false alarms are fine.

3
If you already clicked — still report it

Tell IT what happened, what you clicked, and when. Acting fast limits the damage. There's no judgment — it happens to everyone.

4
Change your password if credentials were entered

If you typed a username or password on a suspicious page, change that password immediately — and any accounts that share the same password.

5
Don't share details with coworkers before IT responds

Well-meaning forwarding of phishing emails can spread the attack. Let IT handle communication.

💬
Remember
Security is a team effort. You are not expected to be an expert — you're expected to stay alert and ask for help when something feels off. No question is too small.
